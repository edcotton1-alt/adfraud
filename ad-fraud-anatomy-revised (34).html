Ad Fraud: The Multi-Billion-Dollar Problem No One Wants to Solve

The anatomy of how fake audiences, fake clicks and fake conversions corrupt the marketing machine, why everyone in the chain profits from looking away, and how the thing filed under marketing waste is quietly becoming plain fraud, at a scale nothing is ready for.

Figures from IAB · ANA · 4A's · WFA · FTC · DOJ · Juniper Research · independent fraud sensors
✓ Verified As Seen On ███████ ★★★★★ 4.9 🔒 100% Secure

Ad fraud is not new. For years, brands, agencies and platforms have wrung their hands over it, published worried reports, vowed to clean it up, and the loss just sat there, a stubborn slice of every digital ad dollar, tolerated as a cost of doing business. Everyone watched it. Almost no one moved.

The numbers are not small, though they are slippery. Count only the clearly fake, the bots and invalid traffic, and it is around a tenth of all digital ad spend; count every dollar that never reaches a real, persuadable human and the estimates run to one in five. Either way the floor is enormous: of the roughly $800bn the world spends on digital advertising each year, blended estimates put $90–100bn on fraud and fake audiences, bots, spoofed sites, hijacked phones, and that figure has nearly tripled in five years. Even on the conservative count, it dwarfs the $33bn the world loses to credit-card fraud (Nilson Report, 2024), the crime we built chips, PINs and fraud hotlines to stop. Ad fraud is bigger, and almost no one is on the line.

Then the tools changed. Cheap GPUs, large language models and AI agents handed the fraudsters an arsenal that used to be out of reach, and the forecasts that had crept up for a decade started to bend toward something steeper. The people who lose are still mostly businesses, the advertiser's budget, with a smaller, uglier slice aimed at you, the scam ads and deepfakes that rob a real person. But the truce that kept it all quietly contained is coming apart.

A problem everyone ignored for years is about to stop being ignorable.

What this piece answers
  1. What actually changed, after a decade of stalemate?
  2. How did the fraudsters win, with everyone watching?
  3. How are they using AI to make their fraud stronger?
  4. What new ground did they move into to find the weak spots?
  5. And what new threats does that pose?

The answer comes in three tenses: what it was and why it held, what it is becoming as the machine flips, and what it will be as the harm spreads. Start with the whole story on one screen.

The whole story: one machine, replaced by another
How it used to work · the stable tax
1A convincing fake, a bot, a fake site, took human time and money to build.
2That cost capped how much fraud could exist. Supply was limited.
3So the loss stayed a survivable tax, roughly one dollar in five.
4A bargain formed: one blind party paid (the advertiser), everyone else profited.
Nobody with the power to fix it had a reason to. So nothing changed.
How it works now · the feedback loop
1AI makes fakes nearly free, one prompt spins up sites, articles, "users."
2The cap is gone, so supply is effectively unlimited.
3And the buying is now an algorithm that rewards whatever "performs", which fraud fakes on demand.
4So the machine sends fraud more money, then trains on the result. The tax compounds.
The bargain breaks: the harm escapes the advertiser, into retailers, consumers, the data itself.

That's the whole argument on one screen. Everything below is the proof, and the receipts. First, how the theft actually works.

How the theft works · one dollar in the machine

A brand pays to reach customers. The money travels through exchanges, resellers and verification tools to reach a page. By the end, some of the "people" are bots, some impressions (the times an ad was supposedly shown) were never actually visible on screen, some clicks were manufactured, and every middleman has taken a fee. The brand gets a dashboard full of success. The customer never existed.

① What it was · the stable tax
The Real Question

If money's tight, why isn't this fixed?

A fifth of digital ad spend buys nothing real. It isn't broken, it works exactly as built: fraud is revenue for everyone in the chain except the one paying. Three actors, three reasons nothing moves.

Why no one fixes ad fraud · 3 of 3The Ledger

Profit, blindness, markup. Those who can see the fraud earn from it; the one who loses can't see. As one Forrester analyst put it, even the victims flinch from looking, "if you cut off the bots and it turns out a large amount of your traffic is bots, that's going to influence your performance numbers." Better, quietly, not to check. So nothing changes, by design.

The platform incentive problem

Sometimes the fraud isn't a botnet. It's a revenue line.

In late 2025 Reuters reported on internal Meta documents. What they showed: reported, disputed

  • ~$16bn of Meta's 2024 revenue (about 10%) projected to come from ads for scams and banned goods.
  • ~15 billion "higher-risk" scam ads shown per day.
  • An internal review: it was "easier to advertise scams on Meta than Google."
  • "Revenue guardrails", enforcement staff barred from anti-fraud actions costing more than a sliver of revenue; advertisers below 95%-certain fraud were charged higher rates, not removed.

Meta calls the $16bn figure "rough and overly-inclusive" and says it removed 134m+ scam ads in 2025. Take the dispute at face value and the question still stands: when a platform earns from the bad ads it polices, is fraud an enforcement problem, or an incentive one?

Which is why no one stops it. Credit-card fraud got chip cards and task forces because the banks demanded it, the loss was a pure cost to them. Ad fraud has no such champion:

  • The platforms who'd lead a crackdown book the disputed spend as revenue. They'll block obvious fraud, but won't invite scrutiny that reclassifies the rest. The fox owns the henhouse.
  • The advertisers' lobby (the ANA) preaches buyer-beware, not regulation, partly because 46 of 67 brands in its own transparency study couldn't even get their own data from their agencies. You can't demand a crackdown on a loss you're not allowed to measure.
  • The advertisers themselves are half-implicated, having chased the cheap inventory where fraud breeds, and the middlemen a regulator would target are the lobby's own members.

So the body that should sound the alarm files careful reports instead, and one of its own analysts' questions, at what point does this require regulation?, hangs there unanswered.

Streaming pays the highest ad rates, so it's the richest target. A cheap off-brand box arrives already compromised, quietly "watches" ads no human sees, and bills the advertiser at premium rates, the screen never even lights up.

And here is the twist that should worry anyone who wants an open internet: the dirtier the open market gets, the more advertisers retreat into the walled gardens. Fraud is lowest inside the big closed platforms, Amazon and Roku among them, so buyers are starting to treat "trusted ecosystem" as a fraud-control feature and flee the open exchanges. vendor data The fraud the giants profit from is also herding everyone deeper inside their walls, which hands still more pricing power and audience data to the same handful of companies the rest of this piece is about.

THE SAME SCHEME, TWO GENERATIONS APART

The clearest measure of what changed isn't a forecast, it's the same family of fraud, before and after the cost of scaling collapsed. The original botnets were already huge. Their successors are larger by an order of magnitude, run on hardware that ships pre-infected, and are now confirmed by law enforcement rather than inferred by a vendor.

● The old baseline (2016–2018)

Methbot rented ~1,900 servers; 3ve commanded 1.7M+ hijacked PCs, the largest of its era, taken down by a DOJ indictment. Building it took real money and human effort, which is what capped how big it could get.

● The 2025–26 successor

BADBOX 2.0: 1M+ infected CTV and IoT devices (up from 74,000 in the original), pushing 750M fraudulent bid requests a day, confirmed by the FBI in June 2025. In North America, bots drove 82% of CTV violations, tuned to mimic a real US household.

Why this is the load-bearing evidence: it's the rare data point with no one selling a cure behind it, a law-enforcement confirmation, on the same scheme, showing a ~13× jump in scale onto devices that arrive compromised. The escalation isn't a projection. It already shipped, in a box, to someone's living room.

② What it is becoming · the deal breaking, now
The Frontier

Fraud doesn't fight fortified ground. It rushes the open gate.

In defended channels, fraud sits at a stubborn, survivable tax: invalid traffic (bots and fake activity, not real people) has held near 9% of programmatic spend since 2023. Google blocked 8.3B bad ads in 2025 and the share didn't move. Record effort, flat result, the nightmare isn't only the fraud nobody sees; it's the fraud everyone sees and still pays for. Because fraud doesn't stay to fight. It moves to wherever just opened and isn't defended yet, and AI made that move nearly free, the "slop-fraud" network DoubleVerify calls AutoBait runs 200+ look-alike "blogs" off one templated AI system, at under $2.25 a page, purely to harvest ad money, and the same slop has spilled off the open web into the feeds, by one study a fifth to a third of what YouTube now recommends is mass-produced AI video. So the real question isn't "how big is the number." It's how deep fraud already is in the two gates that opened in 2026: ads inside AI answers, and AI agents that buy.

The machine learns to pay for fakes

Traditional fraud skimmed a little off the top. The newer sort persuades the machine to hand over more. Advertisers no longer choose where their money goes; they give an algorithm a goal, "find me customers", and let it spend, rewarding whatever looks like success: a click, a sign-up, a sale. Fraud manufactures all three to order. Unable to tell a counterfeit conversion from a real one, the machine decides the fraud is working, sends it more money, and goes hunting for others like it. It does not merely tolerate fraud. It shops for it.

And it now runs the money: roughly 78% of spending on Google Ads, a $224bn-a-year market, is handed to this kind of AI bidding, with Performance Max alone taking a third. The more rope the algorithm gets, the more it hangs you with, let Performance Max choose everything and, by third-party audits, about a quarter of the visitors it buys are fake, several times the rate of an ordinary search ad. In one three-day audit a single campaign burned $2,894 on junk sites and booked 106 fake sign-ups, each teaching it to find more. vendor-audited, disputed

Google's answer is that it filters invalid traffic and doesn't charge advertisers for the fakes it catches, and the loudest fraud figures come from firms that sell the fix. Fair, but it doesn't dissolve the logic: the optimizer still steers toward whatever reports success, and only the fraud it fails to catch ever gets paid for, which is exactly the fraud built to look real.

Two doors just opened, and nobody's guarding them

Fraud always runs to where the defenses haven't arrived. In 2026 two new doors opened at once: ads inside AI answers, and AI agents that shop for you.

The first door: hidden orders the AI obeys. An AI reads a web page and treats everything on it as an instruction. So you can hide an order that a person can't see, but the AI will follow.

It already happened. In December 2025 researchers found a scam page with a secret message aimed at the AI that approves ads. It just said approve this one. No human saw it; the AI did, and said yes. The same trick can feed an AI lies it then repeats as fact, and it can hide on any page, Google scans two to three billion a month and keeps finding the traps. Its own experts say the hole may never fully close.

The second door: the shopper and the scammer look identical. The big shops are wiring themselves into AI assistants, Target, Best Buy, Sephora, Lowe's, Home Depot and others have plugged in so ChatGPT can surface their products, and OpenAI has been building checkout right inside the chat. The rollout has been bumpy, Walmart pulled back from in-chat checkout over accuracy problems, but the direction is set: the AI is moving from suggesting purchases to making them.

A real shopper's AI and a criminal's bot are now the same thing: software holding a credit card. The one question every fraud check was built on, "is there a real person here?", stops working. This kind of shopping traffic jumped almost 8,000% in a year, and by one estimate three in ten retail-fraud attempts are now AI-generated. The fight is already in court: Amazon won an early order against the AI firm Perplexity after its bot logged into people's accounts disguised as a normal browser (an appeals court later paused the order). The nightmare is simple: an AI allowed to ask for refunds, set loose to demand 10,000 in an hour, draining a shop overnight.

The losses are small for now. The openings are not.
③ What it will be · why the stasis can't hold
Why It Breaks Now

The stasis held because the loser couldn't see and no one else cared to look

For thirty years the math of indifference was sound. One party paid, the rest profited, and the bill landed somewhere too murky to read, so doing nothing was the rational move for everyone with the power to act. AI doesn't make that bill bigger so much as it voids the deal that let everyone look away. Three reasons people didn't care, and the specific thing breaking each one.

THE BROKEN DEAL · who was protected, and isn't anymore

The harm is escaping the one party who was paying for it

A tax you can ignore. Contamination of your own decisions, a victim with a lawyer, and a referee made of the same fraud, you cannot.

● The advertiser: the tax becomes data poison

Was: a skim off the top, pay one dollar in five, move on. Now: inverse optimization means the fakes train your buying machine, corrupting its calls on the real 80% too. A CFO tolerates a 20% skim. Not "your automation is learning to burn the rest."

● Everyone else: the victim stops being the budget

Was: the loser was a marketing budget nobody cried over. Now: agentic fraud drains a retailer's cash, a treasury problem. Deepfakes rob consumers, a regulator's problem. Prompt injection hijacks the user's own agent. New victims who can't look away, the thing a thirty-year stalemate was missing.

● The referee: the only check is going synthetic too

Was: flawed measurement, but "good enough." Now: buyer, inventory, audience and verification are all machines fooling each other, and bots have crossed 51% of web traffic. The problem nobody fixed is becoming the one nobody can. Apathy now has a deadline.

Not a new kind of fraud, the taxonomy still holds. What's new is that the economics that made indifference rational are gone, all three terms of the deal failing at once.

The Proportion

A crime this big should be impossible to ignore. It isn't.

Put the yearly loss next to the crimes the world takes seriously, and the strangeness of the silence becomes the story. We built whole defence systems for smaller thefts.

Annual global losses, by crime · what we mobilise against vs what we don't

Ad fraud is roughly three times the size of all the world's credit-card fraud, the crime that earned chip cards, PINs and a permanent enforcement industry, and it is closing on a tenth of the entire global narcotics trade. Yet it has no task force, no hotline, no headlines.

Why the silence? Because the same thing that makes it enormous makes it invisible. The drug trade has bodies and cartels; ad fraud has a fraction-of-a-percent line on ten million separate budgets. No single advertiser loses enough, on any single day, to call the police. It is too big to see precisely because it is spread too thin to hurt, and that diffusion is exactly why the incentive to fix it never arrived.

And who collects? Not lone hackers but organised crews, run like companies, with chief executives, salaried "developers" and shell firms to launder the proceeds, mostly operating out of Russia and Eastern Europe, sharing infrastructure with the same outfits behind state-linked hacking. The man behind Methbot called himself the "king of fraud" and kept three-quarters of the take. His defence at trial is the tell: he insisted he had done nothing wrong, "we were making business, we are not making scam or fraud." The court disagreed and gave him ten years; the half-dozen others charged beside him were never caught. And the telling part is what happened when the industry finally chose to move: once Google and a coalition of rivals coordinated against 3ve, the biggest operation of its kind, its billions of daily fake requests fell almost to zero within eighteen hours. The capability to stop it was never the problem. The will to was. Plenty of people fight ad fraud; almost no one with the power to end it for good has enough reason to.

For thirty years, not caring was the rational move. That's the part that just changed.

The menu is permanent, bot farms, fake followers, counterfeit storefronts, borrowed reputations, and AI is the accelerant, not a new dish. The stasis held for thirty years because the fraud was small enough to absorb, one party paid and everyone else profited, and the loss was spread too thin for anyone to act. It held for one more reason, too: the name. "Ad fraud" tells you where it happens, not what it is. Call a thing by its venue and it sounds like a marketing problem, waste, the cost of doing business online, something for the trade press and the analytics team. Call it by the act, theft and deception at scale, and it belongs on the crime desk. The label was camouflage, and it worked. But it is not really ad fraud any more, if it ever was. AI has stripped out the cost and the labour that used to cap how much of it could exist: the same operation that once faked a few thousand clicks can now generate fake people, fake sites and fake demand by the million, for almost nothing. The thing that kept it contained was never the defences. It was the price of faking, and that price just fell to zero. It was always big. Now it can grow as fast as the machines that run it, and the soft name will not hold much longer.

If you're a small or mid-size brand, seven things to do

You can't audit a chain you're not allowed to see. So don't. Avoid the parts where fraud lives, force the system to chase real money, judge by outcomes a bot can't fake, and watch what the machines now say and do in your name.

  1. Run a "turn-off" test

    Stop all ads in one city or state for two to four weeks and watch whether real sales actually drop. If they hold steady, you were paying for credit on sales that would've happened anyway, not real growth.

    beats attribution fraud
  2. Lock bidding to real cash, not cheap clicks

    Force the platform's algorithm to optimize for things deep in the funnel, cleared payments, verified phone calls, not clicks or form-fills. Optimize for cheap signals and the system will steer your budget straight at the bots that fake them.

    beats inverse optimization
  3. Use hand-vetted inclusion lists

    Switch off the open-web auction entirely. Instead of a blind trail of thousands of junk sites, name a short roster of trusted publishers where you know real people actually read, and spend only there.

    beats made-for-advertising junk
  4. Buy customer identity, not middleman eyeballs

    Take a slice of leaky top-of-funnel budget and give it straight to your audience, an instant discount in exchange for a sign-up. That routes cash away from the ad exchanges and turns anonymous traffic into a relationship you own.

    builds first-party data
  5. Verify at the front gate

    Protect your database with double-opt-in email or a real-time SMS code on high-value actions. AI can fill out a form and mimic human typing flawlessly, but a bot can't easily intercept a code sent to a real phone.

    beats AI slop-fraud
  6. Ask the AI what it says about you

    Once a month, prompt ChatGPT, Gemini and the rest with "is [your brand] legit?" and "[your brand] customer service number." If it returns a wrong number or a smear, a rival or a scammer has likely seeded the web with fakes. The fix is to be the loudest accurate source about yourself, your own site stating the real facts plainly, so the model has clean data to lean on. Watch for the fingerprint of sabotage: clusters of identical, copy-paste negative reviews, real complaints are uneven and specific.

    beats competitor reputation-poisoning
  7. Assume hostile automation, and rate-limit it

    Two machine-speed attacks now come cheap. A rival can rent click-fraud-as-a-service, real platforms exist, styled like normal software, charging around $300 a month with a sales pitch that openly reads "drain your competitors' ad budget automatically", to burn through your daily spend so your ads vanish. And a buying agent can hammer anything you leave open: promo codes, free trials, refunds, bulk orders. A real shopper's agent and a fraud bot look identical, so don't try to tell them apart, cap the action itself: a daily budget ceiling and exclusion lists on your ads, one promo code per verified account, limits on refunds and bulk buys. Make sure nothing can be drained faster than you can notice.

    beats competitor budget-draining + agentic abuse
Notes on the numbers

How much is real?

Treat every total on this page as directional. No one sees the whole picture, each measurer catches only a slice. Dr. Augustine Fou, the field's leading skeptic, won't give an industry-wide figure at all, noting that detection vendors profit from the problem they measure; from the other side, former Meta integrity chief Rob Leathern says it's impossible to know how bad it's gotten because platforms won't open their data. So estimates swing from single digits to 90%. The case it's under-counted: detection tools miss attribution fraud, claiming credit for sales that would've happened anyway. The case it's over-counted: the vendors sizing the problem are the ones selling the cure. The one thing that cuts through it is the controlled test, switch the spend off and see if sales drop.

Note on rigour: prosecuted-case and settlement figures (Methbot/3ve, Facebook, Meta suits, FTC, the FBI's BADBOX confirmation, Google's blocked-ad count) are the firmest. The aggregate totals ($88B, ~$100B, $172B projected) and the industry-audit, sensor and projection figures (Juniper, ANA, IAS, DoubleVerify, Spider Labs and others) are directional, blend different scopes and definitions, and several originate with vendors that sell fraud-detection services, interested, though not therefore wrong. No single body tallies every type, so any "total" is a blended estimate, not a clean sum. Meta disputes the Reuters scam-ad figures as "rough and overly inclusive" and points to its own enforcement numbers; both sides are represented. This page is framed around US impact, US budgets, US victims, US platforms and regulators, while noting that perpetrators are often offshore. The inverse-optimization mechanism is documented by vendors and by Google's own by-channel fraud rates, but is not yet a controlled causal study; the agentic-commerce harms are a leading edge, real attack traffic but immaterial realized losses so far, and are presented as trajectory, not tally. Anthropic's ad-free position is its own stated policy. 2026 platform-revenue and AI-fraud figures are fast-moving reporting.
